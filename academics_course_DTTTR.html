Designing Technological Tools for Thinking and Learning

How do kids (or adults for that matter) actually learn? How is learning changing in a world that is growing ever more technological? How can we as educators embrace technology, and even use it to improve pedagogy and learning outcomes?

All of these questions, and more, were explored in this course taught by Professor Uri Wilensky, inventor of NetLogo. The course’s foundation lay in the idea of Constructionist Learning, a concept first introduced by Seymour Papert, which says that students learn most effectively through discovery and interaction with the world around them. Constructionist theories laid the foundation for many of the experiential learning practices that have been embraced in many educational environments today.

As a part of this project focused course, we designed and built our own interactive platforms and curriculums, rooted in Constructionism’s principles, for integrating technology into learning. My project, along with my partner Kritphong Mongkhonvanit, was designing and building a hybrid simulation and physical robotics system to help teach fundamental neuroscience and biology to young learners. The idea stemmed from the first few chapters of Valentino Braitenberg’s book Vehicles, which discussed how even simple neural connections can lead to insect-like behaviors.

The final curriculum designed for the course’s final project can be found here, along with the poster presentation.

We also submitted the project as a demo paper to the Interaction Design and Children (IDC ‘24) Conference. Check out the IDC Paper here!
